The Hey Bradley Story

Told through the StoryBrand framework: every great product is a story about the customer.

"

Sarah owns a bakery in Portland. She makes the best sourdough in the Pearl District, and her grandmother's cinnamon roll recipe has a two-hour wait on Saturdays. She needed a website. She tried Wix -- it looked like every other template bakery site on the internet. She hired a freelance developer, but the specs were a disaster: "make it responsive" meant different things to everyone in the room, "warm feel" turned into a beige rectangle, and three rounds of revisions later she had spent $4,000 on a site that still did not feel like her bakery.

Sarah was stuck in the gap that swallows millions of small businesses every year: the space between vibe coding (pretty but fragile, impossible to maintain or hand off) and enterprise specifications (powerful but so complex that only a senior architect can write them). She did not need a drag-and-drop toy. She did not need a 200-page requirements document. She needed someone to listen to what she wanted and translate it into something a developer -- or an AI -- could actually build.

-- The problem Hey Bradley solves

1

The Character

Sarah is not a designer, and she should not have to be. She knows her bakery's story -- the family recipes, the Saturday morning lines, the smell of fresh bread at 5 AM. She knows what she wants her website to feel like. But she does not speak CSS, and she cannot describe a "hero section with radial gradient overlay" any more than her developer can describe the difference between poolish and biga.

2

The Problem

External: Every handoff between Sarah, a designer, and a developer introduces ambiguity. "Make it warm" becomes a game of telephone.
Internal: Sarah feels stupid. She second-guesses herself, settles for a generic Wix template, or burns through revision cycles at $150/hour.
Philosophical: It should not take a computer science degree to get the website you imagined.

3

The Guide

Hey Bradley is a digital whiteboard that listens. Sarah tells it about her bakery -- the grandmother's recipes, the Saturday crowds, the warm amber lighting -- and it generates both a live preview she can see and tweak, AND enterprise-grade specifications (AISP 5.1) that reduce ambiguity from 40-65% down to under 2%. It translates her vision into a language that both humans and machines understand perfectly.

4

The Plan

Step 1: Tell Bradley about your business. "I need a bakery website with a warm feel, photos of my pastries, online ordering, and a story about my grandmother's recipes."
Step 2: Watch it build your site in real-time. Drag sections, swap themes, refine until it feels right.
Step 3: Get specs that any developer (or AI) can reproduce. Six enterprise-grade documents are generated automatically.

5

The Call to Action

Try the canned demo -- speak or type, watch the magic. The demo is live, deployed on every push to main. No signup, no credit card, no developer required. Just describe what you want and see it appear.

6

Success

Sarah's bakery website looks like a $10,000 agency built it -- warm tones, beautiful pastry gallery, online ordering, her grandmother's story front and center. AND she has specs so precise that Claude Code reproduces the site at 90% fidelity on the first try. No ambiguity. No "that is not what I meant." No wasted revision cycles.

7

Failure (Without It)

Without Hey Bradley, Sarah stays in vibe coding limbo -- a nice-looking website that breaks when she tries to add a page, that no developer can maintain because there are no specs, and that would need to be completely rebuilt when she opens her second location. The gap between "what I want" and "what I got" remains the most expensive problem in web development.

AISP 5.1 Platinum -- Crystal Atom Notation

The AI-Interpretable Site Protocol is the core innovation: a structured notation that encodes every detail of a website in a format optimized for both human review and machine parsing. Traditional specs leave 40-65% of implementation details ambiguous. AISP reduces that to under 2%.

How Crystal Atom Works

Every site element is encoded as a "Crystal Atom" -- a self-contained unit with typed bindings that specify exact values for every visual and structural property. The notation uses Greek-letter prefixes for binding categories (λ for layout, Σ for style, Δ for data) and mathematical operators for relationships between atoms.

The result: an AI reading an AISP spec knows exactly what to build. No interpretation needed. No "I think the designer meant..." moments. Every pixel, every spacing value, every color token is explicitly declared.

Agentic Development Process

Hey Bradley was built using Claude Code multi-agent swarms: 12-18 AI agents per phase, coordinated in a hierarchical mesh topology, with a 3-loop optimization cycle and brutally honest peer reviews.

1. Planning & Specification

Each phase begins with a detailed spec: checklist items, Definition of Done criteria, scoring rubric. The planner agent breaks work into atomic tasks assigned to specialized agents.

2-3 agents: planner, researcher, architect

2. Parallel Implementation

6-8 coder agents execute in parallel. Each owns a bounded context (templates, sections, specs, tests). Agents read shared memory for coordination and write results to a common namespace.

6-8 agents: coders, testers, reviewers

3. Review & Scoring

A 12-agent audit swarm reviews every change: code quality, test coverage, accessibility, performance, and adherence to ADRs. Scores are brutally honest -- no inflation.

12 agents: full audit swarm

4. 3-Loop Optimization

Loop 1: Fix all critical issues. Loop 2: Improve quality to target score. Loop 3: Polish and document. Each loop is a full plan-execute-review cycle. The process stops when the phase score meets the bar or diminishing returns are reached.

3 cycles per phase, 15-18 agents total

5. Documentation & ADRs

Every architectural decision is captured in an ADR (Architecture Decision Record). 22 ADRs document choices like component structure, state management, theming strategy, and the AISP notation design.

1-2 agents: documenter, reviewer

6. Retrospective & Backlog

Each phase closes with a retrospective: what worked, what did not, what to carry forward. Technical debt is logged, prioritized, and tracked. Living checklists ensure nothing is forgotten.

Session logs, wikis, backlog updates
Key insight: Agentic development is not about speed -- it is about consistency. Human developers have good days and bad days. A swarm of 15 agents, each with a clear role and shared context, produces remarkably consistent output across phases. The quality bar is enforced by the scoring rubric, not by human willpower.

About Bradley Ross

Bradley Ross

Harvard ALM candidate, Digital Media Design -- Extension School

Creator of the AISP (AI-Interpretable Site Protocol) and the Hey Bradley platform. Bradley's capstone research focuses on the intersection of natural language processing, web development automation, and enterprise specification standards. The central thesis: if you can eliminate ambiguity from website specifications, you can enable AI agents to reproduce any site design with near-perfect fidelity.

Hey Bradley is the practical demonstration of this thesis -- a platform where non-technical users describe what they want, see it live, and receive specifications detailed enough for 90% reproduction by Claude Code or any competent developer. The project has been built entirely using agentic development: multi-agent AI swarms coordinated via Claude Code, with every architectural decision documented in one of 22 ADRs.

The AISP 5.1 Platinum protocol -- specifically the Crystal Atom notation -- is the core intellectual contribution. It provides a formal, typed notation for encoding website structure and design that reduces specification ambiguity from the industry standard of 40-65% to under 2%.
